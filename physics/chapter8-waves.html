Chapter 8: Waves

  • Introduction to Waves

    Show/Hide Details
    • Wave: Disturbance created in a medium.
    • Waves transport energy without transporting matter.
    • Waves transport both energy and momentum in a medium.

    • Classification of Waves

      Show/Hide Details
      • Mechanical Waves: Require a medium for propagation (e.g., sound waves, string waves, water waves).
      • Electromagnetic Waves: Do not require a medium for propagation (e.g., radio waves, heat waves, light waves).
      • Matter Waves: Associated with moving particles.

    • Progressive/Traveling Waves

      Show/Hide Details
      • Propagates or distributes pulses in space along a specific direction.
      • Examples: Waves on a string, waves on a water surface.

    • Periodic Waves

      Show/Hide Details
      • Repeated in regular intervals of time.
      • May be transverse or longitudinal.

      Transverse Waves:

      • Displacement of medium is perpendicular to wave propagation.
      • Examples: Ripple in a pond, wave on a string.
      • Cannot propagate in gases or liquids.

      Longitudinal Waves:

      • Displacement of medium is parallel to wave propagation.
      • Examples: Wave in a spring, sound waves in air.
    • For all waves: $v = f\lambda$.
    • Particles separated by $n\lambda$ are in phase.
    • Particles separated by $(n+\frac{1}{2})\lambda$ are out of phase.

  • Sound Waves

    Show/Hide Details
    • Produced by vibrating bodies ($\lambda \approx 1m$).
    • Essentials for sound: Vibrating body, medium for propagation, listener.
    • Longitudinal waves with 3D propagation in air.
    • Consist of compressions (max crowding) and rarefactions (min crowding).
    • Exhibit Reflection, Refraction, Diffraction, Interference, but NOT Polarization (because they are longitudinal).

    • Velocity of Sound in Air

      Show/Hide Details
      • Speed of any mechanical wave: $v=\sqrt{\frac{E}{\rho}}$. ($E$: modulus of elasticity, $\rho$: density).
      • For gases (compressional wave): $v=\sqrt{\frac{E_{bulk}}{\rho}}$.
      • Newton's formula (isothermal assumption): $v=\sqrt{\frac{P}{\rho}}$.
        • At S.T.P: $v \approx 281~m/s$.
        • Experimental value: $332~m/s$.
        • Percentage error in Newton's calculation: 16%.
      • Laplace's formula (adiabatic assumption): $v=\sqrt{\frac{\gamma P}{\rho}}$.
        • $\gamma = \frac{C_p}{C_v} = 1.42$ (for air).
      • Speed increases by $0.61~m/s$ (or $61~cm/s$) for each $1^\circ C$ rise in temperature: $v_t = v_o + 0.61t$.
      • Effect of Moisture: Presence of moisture reduces air density, increasing sound speed.
      • Dependence:
        • Independent of pressure.
        • $v \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{\rho}}$.
        • $v \propto \sqrt{T}$.
        • $\frac{v_1}{v_2} = \sqrt{\frac{T_1}{T_2}}$.
      • Speed of sound in hydrogen is 4 times more than in oxygen at same temperature.
      WAVE SPEED (m/s)
      EM waves 300,000,000
      Sound in air 340
      Sound in water 1500
      Sound in steel 5000

  • Principle of Superposition

    Show/Hide Details
    • When two or more waves reach a point simultaneously, the resultant displacement is the sum of individual displacements.
    • $Y_{total} = Y_1 + Y_2 + Y_3 + ... + Y_n$.

    • Applications of Superposition

      Show/Hide Details
      • Interference: Superposition of two waves of same frequency, traveling in same direction.
      • Beats: Produced by two waves of slightly different frequencies traveling in the same direction.
      • Stationary Waves: Produced by two waves of equal frequency and amplitude traveling along same line in opposite direction.

  • Interference of Sound

    Show/Hide Details
    • Mixing of two identical sound waves propagating along same direction.
    • Conditions: coherent waves, same medium, same direction, identical waves, sources close.
    • Constructive Interference: Waves reinforce, louder sound.
      • Condition: Path difference $= n\lambda$ (where $n=0, \pm1, \pm2, ...$).
      • Echoing zone.
    • Destructive Interference: Waves cancel, fainter sound.
      • Condition: Path difference $= (n+\frac{1}{2})\lambda$ (where $n=0, \pm1, \pm2, ...$).
      • Silence zone.
    • Path difference: Difference between lengths of paths traveled by two waves.

  • Beats

    Show/Hide Details
    • Periodic alterations of sound between maximum and minimum loudness.
    • Produced by superposition of two waves with slightly different frequencies.
    • Beat frequency ($f_{beat}$): Number of beats per second.
    • $f_{beat} = |f_A - f_B|$.
    • Maximum detectable beat frequency for normal human ear is 10Hz.
    • Uses: Determine unknown frequencies, tune musical instruments.

  • Reflection of Waves

    Show/Hide Details
    • All kinds of waves show reflection.
    • From denser boundary: Phase reverses ($180^\circ$ change). Reflection coefficient maximum, transmission coefficient almost zero.
    • From rarer boundary: No phase reversal. Reflection and transmission coefficients have considerable values.
    • Reflected wave has same wavelength and frequency, but phase may change.

  • Transmission of Waves

    Show/Hide Details
    • Amplitude of transmitted wave is less than incident wave (energy loss).
    • Frequency of transmitted wave is same as incident wave.
    • Velocity depends on density of medium: $\nu \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{\rho}}$.
    • Wavelength changes because velocity changes while frequency remains constant.

  • Standing Waves (Stationary Waves)

    Show/Hide Details
    • Superposition of two identical waves traveling opposite to each other in the same medium.
    • Involves both constructive and destructive interference.
    • Antinodes: Points of constructive interference (maximum amplitude). Energy is wholly potential at extreme position, wholly kinetic at equilibrium.
    • Nodes: Points of destructive interference (minimum/zero amplitude), stationary points.
    • Distance between two consecutive nodes or antinodes: $\lambda/2$.
    • Distance between a node and consecutive antinode: $\lambda/4$.

    • Standing Waves in Stretched String

      Show/Hide Details
      • Fixed end: always a node. Free end: always an antinode.
      • String fixed at both ends: Number of nodes = Number of antinodes + 1 ($N=A+1$).
      • String free at one end: Number of antinodes = Number of nodes.
      • Speed of string wave: $v = \sqrt{\frac{T}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{F}{m}}$ ($m$: linear mass density).
      • Speed of stationary waves is independent of number of loops.
      • Quantized frequencies: $f_n = n f_1$ (where $n=1,2,3,...$).
      • Fundamental frequency: $f_1 = \frac{1}{2l}\sqrt{\frac{T}{m}}$.
      • Overtones/Harmonics: Integral multiples of fundamental frequency.

    • Standing Waves in Air Columns (Pipes)

      Show/Hide Details
      • Open end: Antinode. Closed end: Node.
      Pipes open or closed at both ends / strings fixed at both ends Pipes closed at one end
      Length L $L = n\frac{\lambda}{2}$ $L = (2n-1)\frac{\lambda}{4}$
      Fundamental $f = v/2L$ $f = v/4L$
      Harmonics $2f, 3f, ... , nf$ $3f, 5f, ... , (2n-1)f$
      • Fundamental frequency of open pipe = $2 \times$ fundamental frequency of closed pipe.
      • Number of harmonics in open pipe = $2 \times$ Number of harmonics in closed pipe.

  • Doppler's Effect

    Show/Hide Details
    • Apparent change in pitch (frequency) of sound due to relative motion of source and observer.
    • Discovered by Doppler in 1845.
    • $v$: true speed of sound. $u_o$: speed of listener. $u_s$: speed of source.

    • Formulas for Apparent Frequency ($f'$)

      Show/Hide Details
      • Source moving towards stationary listener: $f' = \left(\frac{v}{v-u_s}\right)f$ ($f' > f$).
      • Source moving away from stationary listener: $f' = \left(\frac{v}{v+u_s}\right)f$ ($f' < f$).
      • Listener moving towards stationary source: $f' = \left(\frac{v+u_o}{v}\right)f$ ($f' > f$).
      • Listener moving away from stationary source: $f' = \left(\frac{v-u_o}{v}\right)f$ ($f' < f$).
      • Source and listener moving towards each other: $f' = \left(\frac{v+u_o}{v-u_s}\right)f$.
      • Source and observer moving away from each other: $f' = \left(\frac{v-u_o}{v+u_s}\right)f$.

    • Applications of Doppler's Effect

      Show/Hide Details
      • Ships and submarines (sonar devices).
      • Bats (for traveling).
      • Radar (for detection).
      • Determining velocity of a star w.r.t. Earth.
      • Monitoring blood flow in major arteries.
      • Red shift: Wavelength of light increases when star moves away from Earth.
      • Blue shift: Wavelength of light decreases when star moves towards Earth.
