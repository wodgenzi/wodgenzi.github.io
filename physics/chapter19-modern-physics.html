Chapter 19: Dawn of Modern Physics

  • Classical Physics vs. Modern Physics

    Show/Hide Details
    Classical Physics Modern Physics
    Based on classical/Newtonian mechanics. Based on quantum mechanics.
    Deals with macroscopic level, ordinary everyday life. Deals with microscopic level, and macro levels as special cases.
    Span includes: Newton's laws of motion, Newton's law of gravitation, Four laws of thermodynamics, Gas laws, Maxwell's equations of E.M. waves. Span includes: Michelson's and Morley's findings, Schrodinger wave equations, De-Broglie hypothesis, Bohr's ideas, Heisenberg's uncertainty principle, Planck's theory, Special theory of relativity, General theory of relativity.

  • Ideas of Modern Physics

    Show/Hide Details
    • Light is an electromagnetic wave.
    • Light consists of photons, each having energy $E=hf$.
    • Speed of light is a universal constant in vacuum, independent of motion of source and observers.
    • Absorption and emission of electromagnetic waves take place in packets of energy called photons.
    • True laws of physics retain their mathematical form in all inertial frames of reference.
    • All motions and rest are relative; nothing like absolute rest or motion.
    • Wave-particle duality.
    • Reaction takes some time to replay, after action.
    • Matter waves are associated with moving particles.
    • Einstein's Mass-Energy relation: $E=mc^2$.
    • Pair production: Photon splits into electron-positron pair.
    • Positron and electron annihilate to photons.
    • Position, momentum, energy, and time cannot be measured accurately at the same time (Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle).

  • Relative Motion & Frame of Reference

    Show/Hide Details
    • Concept of direction is purely relative.
    • Experiments give same results in same frame of reference; different results in different frames.
    • Frame of Reference: Coordinate system (x, y, z axes) from where observation is made.
    • Inertial Frame: At rest or moving with uniform velocity. Law of inertia is valid. Earth can be considered an inertial frame for motion on Earth.
    • Non-inertial Frame: Moving with accelerated motion.

  • Theory of Relativity (Special Theory)

    Show/Hide Details
    • Proposed by Einstein in 1905.
    • Deals with inertial frames of reference.
    • Important Results:
      • Relativistic Mass: $m=\frac{m_o}{\sqrt{1-\frac{v^2}{c^2}}}$. ($m_o$: rest mass, $v$: relativistic speed, $c$: speed of light).
      • Lorentz Contraction (Length Contraction): $l=l_o\sqrt{1-\frac{v^2}{c^2}}$. ($l_o$: proper length).
      • Time Dilation: $t=\frac{t_o}{\sqrt{1-\frac{v^2}{c^2}}}$. ($t_o$: proper time).
      • Mass-Energy Equivalence: $E=mc^2$.
      • No body moves with velocity greater than velocity of light.
      • At ordinary speeds ($v \approx 0$), $m \approx m_o$, $l \approx l_o$, $t \approx t_o$. This is why relativistic effects are not observed in daily life.
      • If $v = 0.999c$, then $m = 22.4 m_o$.
      • If $v \ge 2.6 \times 10^8~m/s$, then $l = l_o/2$ and $m = 2 m_o$.
    • NAVSTAR Navigation System uses results of special theory of relativity for accurate location and speed measurements.

  • Black Body Radiation

    Show/Hide Details
    • Black body: Ideal radiator and ideal absorber. Emissive/absorptive power is 1. Very bad reflector.
    • Emits electromagnetic waves.
    • Intensity Distribution (Lummer and Pringsheim): Energy is not uniformly distributed across wavelengths at a given temperature.
    • Wien's Displacement Law: $\lambda_{max} T = \text{constant} = 2.9 \times 10^{-3}~m K$. So, $T \propto \frac{1}{\lambda}$. (Holds for shorter wavelengths).
    • Stefan-Boltzmann Law: Total energy (area under curve) $E = \sigma T^4$. ($\sigma = 5.67 \times 10^{-8}~W m^{-2} K^{-4}$ is Stefan's constant).
    • Shape of black body radiation curve is independent of material.
    • Classical theories (Wein's, Rayleigh-Jeans) failed to explain the full spectrum. Rayleigh-Jeans holds for longer wavelengths.
    • Planck's Quantum Theory: Absorption and emission of radiation takes place in packets of energy called quanta ($E=hf$). Explains black body radiation at all wavelengths. Max Planck received Nobel Prize in 1918.

  • The Photons

    Show/Hide Details
    • Einstein: Light energy consists of packets of electromagnetic energy (photons).
    • Planck: Emission and absorption by atoms is in form of indivisible packets (quanta).
    • Light has discontinuous (granular) nature.
    • Beam of light with wavelength $\lambda$ consists of stream of photons traveling at speed $c$ and carrying energy $hf$.
    • Energy: $E = hf = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$.
    • Momentum: $p = mc = \frac{hf}{c} = \frac{h}{\lambda}$.
    • Emission or absorption of energy applies to any oscillating system.

  • Interaction of Electromagnetic Radiation with Matter

    Show/Hide Details
    • Three types depending on photon energy:
    • Energy Range Type of Interaction
      $hf < 0.1~MeV$ Photoelectric effect
      $0.1~MeV < hf < 1~MeV$ Compton effect
      $hf > 1~MeV$ Pair production

    • Photoelectric Effect & Photon Theory of Light

      Show/Hide Details
      • Emission of electrons from a metal surface when light is incident on it.
      • Supports quantum nature of light. Emitted electrons are photoelectrons.
      • Based on energy conservation. Explained by Einstein (Nobel Prize 1921).
      • Photoelectric current ($i$) is proportional to intensity of light ($I$). ($i \propto I$).
      • Saturation current: Maximum photoelectric current at fixed positive potential.
      • Stopping potential ($-V_o$): Negative potential at which photoelectric current becomes zero.
      • Stopping potential is proportional to maximum kinetic energy ($K.E_{max}$) of photoelectrons.
      • Stopping potential depends on frequency of incident light, NOT on intensity.
      • Einstein's Photoelectric Equation: $K.E_{max} = hf - \phi_o = hf - hf_o = h(f-f_o)$.
      • Also, $eV_o = K.E_{max} = \frac{1}{2}mv_{max}^2$.
      • Laws of Photoelectric Effect:
        1. Rate of emission $\propto$ intensity of incident light.
        2. Existence of threshold frequency ($f_o$): Minimum frequency below which no electrons are ejected, regardless of intensity.
        3. Work function ($\phi_o$): Minimum energy to eject an electron. $\phi_o = hf_o = \frac{hc}{\lambda_o}$. Depends on nature of metal and impurities. Unit: eV, Joule, erg.
        4. Maximum K.E. of photoelectrons $\propto$ frequency of incident light, independent of intensity.
        5. Instantaneous process: Time interval between light incidence and electron emission is negligible ($<10^{-8}s$).
      • Failure of Classical Theory: Could not explain independence of K.E. from intensity, existence of threshold frequency, and instantaneous emission.

    • Photocell

      Show/Hide Details
      • Based on photoelectric effect.
      • Consists of cathode (photo-emissive surface) and anode.
      • Sodium/Potassium for visible light, Cesium coated oxidized silver for infrared, Selenium/Silicon for UV/X-rays/gamma-rays.
      • Applications: Security systems, Counting systems, Automatic doors/street lighting, Exposure meters, Sound tracks of movies, Solar panels.

    • Compton Effect

      Show/Hide Details
      • When a photon hits an electron, it scatters with a frequency less than incident photon (wavelength increases).
      • Usually X-ray photons used ($E \ge 17.5~KeV$).
      • Compton shift ($\Delta\lambda$): Increase in wavelength.
      • $\Delta\lambda = \lambda_f - \lambda_i = \frac{h}{m_o c}(1-\cos\theta)$.
      • Compton wavelength: $\frac{h}{m_o c} = 2.43 \times 10^{-12}~m$.
      • $\Delta\lambda = 0$ when $\theta = 0^\circ$.
      • $\Delta\lambda = \frac{h}{m_o c}$ when $\theta = 90^\circ$.
      • $\Delta\lambda = \frac{2h}{m_o c}$ when $\theta = 180^\circ$.
      • Strong evidence for particle (photon) nature of E.M. waves.

    • Pair Production

      Show/Hide Details
      • Decomposition of a photon into an electron-positron pair.
      • Requires photon energy $hf > 1.02~MeV$.
      • Energy equation: $hf = 2m_o c^2 + K.E_{e^-} + K.E_{e^+}$.
      • Rest mass energy of electron/positron = $m_o c^2 = 0.51~MeV$.
      • Cannot take place in vacuum; usually occurs near a heavy nucleus (for momentum conservation).
      • Materialization of energy.

    • Annihilation of Matter

      Show/Hide Details
      • Reverse process of pair production. Conversion of mass into energy.
      • Electron and positron annihilate to produce two photons.
      • Two photons move in opposite directions to conserve momentum.
      • Each photon has energy $0.51~MeV$ (equivalent to rest mass energy of electron).

      Show/Hide Details

      • Every antiparticle has same mass, same spin, but opposite magnetic moment and charge to its respective particle.

  • De-Broglie's Hypothesis (Wave-Particle Duality)

    Show/Hide Details
    • All moving particles behave as waves (matter waves or particle waves).
    • Wavelength associated with moving particles: $\lambda = \frac{h}{mv} = \frac{h}{p}$.
    • $\lambda \propto \frac{1}{m}$, $\lambda \propto \frac{1}{v}$.
    • For large mass and ordinary speed objects, wavelength is too small for wave effects (interference, diffraction) to be observable.
    • Prince Louis Victor de Broglie received 1929 Nobel Prize.

    • Davisson and Germer Experiment

      Show/Hide Details
      • Experimental confirmation of de-Broglie's hypothesis.
      • Showed electrons are diffracted from metal crystals like X-rays.
      • Wavelength associated with moving electrons: $\lambda = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2mVe}}$. ($V$: accelerating potential).
      • For $V=54~V$, $\lambda = 1.66 \times 10^{-10}~m$.
      • This matches Bragg's equation for 1st order diffraction from nickel crystal ($d=0.91 \times 10^{-10}~m$), which gives $\lambda = 1.65 \times 10^{-10}~m$.
      • Davisson and Thomson shared Nobel Prize in 1937.
      • Electron microscope is a practical application of wave-particle duality.

  • Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle

    Show/Hide Details
    • Certain pairs of quantities cannot be measured with perfect accuracy at the same time.
    • Conjugate quantities: Linear momentum and position ($\Delta p \cdot \Delta x \approx h$). Energy and time ($\Delta E \cdot \Delta t \approx h$).
    • To increase accuracy in measuring position, use short wavelength.
    • To increase accuracy in measuring momentum, use long wavelength.
