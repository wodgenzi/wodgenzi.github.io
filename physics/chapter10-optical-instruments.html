Chapter 10: Optical Instruments

  • Lens

    Show/Hide Details
    • Transparent medium bounded by two surfaces, at least one of which is curved.
    • Every lens is part of some sphere.

    • Types of Lenses

      Show/Hide Details

      Convex Lens (Converging Lens):

      • Thicker at the middle, thinner at the edges.
      • Positive focal length.

      Concave Lens (Diverging Lens):

      • Thinner at the middle, thicker at the edges.
      • Negative focal length.
      Sr. No. Convex Lens Concave Lens
      1 Double convex Double concave
      2 Plano convex Plano concave
      3 Concavo convex Convexo concave

    • Important Definitions

      Show/Hide Details
      • Centre of Curvature ($C_1, C_2$): Centre of sphere from which lens surface is obtained. Every lens has two centers of curvature.
      • Radius of Curvature ($R_1, R_2$): Radius of sphere from which lens surface is obtained. Every lens has two radii of curvature.
      • Principal Axis: Line joining the two centers of curvature.
      • Principal Focus (F):
        • For convex lens: Point of convergence of refracted light ray (real point).
        • For concave lens: Point from which refracted light ray appears to diverge (imaginary point).
        • Every lens has two foci, one on each side.
      • Optical Centre (O): Point inside the body of lens through which light rays pass undeviated.
      • Focal Length (f): Distance between principal focus and optical center. Positive for convex, negative for concave.
      • Aperture: Size of diameter of lens.
      • Power of a Lens (P): Ability to deviate light ray from its original path.
        • $P = \frac{1}{f}$ (where $f$ is focal length in meters).
        • If $f$ is in cm, $P = \frac{100}{f}$.
        • SI unit: Dioptre (D). $1~D = 1~m^{-1}$.

    • Combination of Lenses

      Show/Hide Details
      • General rule for combination of 'n' lenses: $P = P_1 + P_2 + ... + P_n$.
      • $\frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{f_1} + \frac{1}{f_2} + ... + \frac{1}{f_n}$.
      • For two double convex lenses: $P = P_1 + P_2$, $\frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{f_1} + \frac{1}{f_2} = \frac{f_1+f_2}{f_1f_2} \implies f = \frac{f_1f_2}{f_1+f_2}$. (Behaves as a convex lens).
      • For two concave lenses: $P = P_1 + P_2$, $\frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{-f_1} + \frac{1}{-f_2} = -\frac{(f_1+f_2)}{f_1f_2} \implies f = -\frac{f_1f_2}{f_1+f_2}$. (Behaves as a concave lens).
      • For one convex and one concave lens: $\frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{f_1} + \frac{1}{-f_2} = \frac{f_2-f_1}{f_1f_2} \implies f = \frac{f_1f_2}{f_2-f_1}$.

    • Lens Formula

      Show/Hide Details
      • $\frac{1}{p} + \frac{1}{q} = \frac{1}{f}$.
      • $p$: object distance (positive for real object, negative for virtual object).
      • $q$: image distance (positive for real image, negative for virtual image).
      • $f$: focal length (positive for convex, negative for concave).
      • Positive sign is used where rays actually intersect. If not, negative sign.

      Position and Nature of Image for Convex Lens:

      Object Position of Image Nature of Image
      Beyond 2F Between F and 2F Real, inverted, small
      At 2F At 2F Real, inverted, equal
      Between F and 2F Beyond 2F Real, inverted, enlarged
      At F At infinity Real, inverted, enlarged
      Inside F On same side further away from lens than the object Virtual, erect, enlarged

    • Magnification of a Lens (M)

      Show/Hide Details
      • Linear magnification: $M = \frac{q}{p} = \frac{h_i}{h_o}$ (size of image / size of object).
      • Angular magnification: $M_a = \frac{\text{Angle formed at aided eye}}{\text{Angle formed at naked eye}}$.
      • For real image: $M = \frac{q}{p}$. For virtual image: $M = -\frac{q}{p}$.
      • For very small angles, linear and angular magnifications can be equated.

    • Visual Angle

      Show/Hide Details
      • Angle made by object at observer's eye.
      • Apparent size of object varies with visual angle.

    • Resolving Power

      Show/Hide Details
      • Ability of an instrument to reveal the minor details of the object under examination.
      • Angle of Resolution ($\alpha_{min}$): Minimum angle that allows two point sources to appear distinctly separated.
      • $\alpha_{min} = 1.22 \frac{\lambda}{D}$.
      • Rayleigh showed that resolving power of a lens of aperture $D$, under a light source of wavelength $\lambda$ is $R = \frac{D}{1.22\lambda}$.

    • Lens Aberrations

      Show/Hide Details
      • Spherical Aberration: Blurred image due to size of lens (outer rays undergo more refraction). Remedy: Use stops or combine convex and concave lenses.
      • Chromatic Aberration: Inability of a lens to bring all light rays (all colors) at single focus. Remedy: Use achromatic lenses.

    • Least Distance of Distinct Vision

      Show/Hide Details
      • Minimum distance at which a normal eye can see an object clearly.
      • $d = 25~cm$.

  • Optical Instruments

    Show/Hide Details
    • Simple microscope, Compound microscope, Telescope, Spectrometer, Camera, Periscope.

    • Simple Microscope (Magnifying Glass)

      Show/Hide Details
      • Double convex lens of short focal length.
      • Magnifying power: $M = 1 + \frac{d}{f}$.
      • Forms virtual, erect, and magnified image at $d=25~cm$.

    • Compound Microscope

      Show/Hide Details
      • Consists of two convex lenses:
        • Objective lens: small size, short focal length.
        • Eyepiece lens: large size, large focal length.
      • Objective forms real, inverted, magnified image. Eyepiece forms virtual, erect, magnified image of objective's image.
      • Magnification: $M = \frac{L}{f_o}\left(1+\frac{d}{f_e}\right)$.
      • $L$: distance between lenses. $f_o$: objective focal length. $f_e$: eyepiece focal length.
      • Magnification of objective: $\frac{L}{f_o}$. Magnification of eyepiece: $1+\frac{d}{f_e}$.
      • A wider objective and use of blue light of short wavelength produces less diffraction and allows more details to be seen.

    • Telescope

      Show/Hide Details
      • Optical instrument to see fine details of a far off object.
      • Two major classes: Reflecting and Refracting.

      Astronomical Telescope:

      • Consists of two lenses: Objective (large focal length, large aperture), Eyepiece (small focal length, small aperture).
      • For infinity/parallel rays, $L = f_o + f_e$.
      • Advantages: High magnifying power ($M = \frac{f_o}{f_e}$), Large field of view.
      • Disadvantage: Final image is inverted.

      Galilean Telescope:

      • Uses a positive objective and a negative eyepiece.
      • Gives erect images and is shorter than astronomical telescope.

    • Defects of Vision

      Show/Hide Details
      • Myopia (Short-Sightedness or Near-Sightedness): Distant objects are not clearly visible. Image formed before retina. Remedy: Concave lens (negative focal length/power).
      • Hyperopia (Long-Sightedness or Far-Sightedness): Near objects are not clearly visible. Image formed behind retina. Remedy: Convex lens (positive focal length/power).
      • Presbyopia: Old age defect where eye muscles lose elasticity. Remedy: Bifocal lenses.
      • Astigmatism: Eye defect where vision is blurred due to irregular curvature of cornea or lens.

    • Spectrometer

      Show/Hide Details
      • Optical instrument used to study spectrum of light.
      • Components: Collimator (produces parallel beam), Telescope (astronomical telescope), Turn Table (prism or diffraction grating), Leveling Screws.
      • Working:
        • Telescope is set for infinity and fixed.
        • Source of monochromatic light is placed in front of slit and collimator is adjusted for parallel rays.
        • Angle of minimum deviation ($D_m$) is experimentally determined.
      • Refractive index of prism: $n = \frac{\sin\left(\frac{A+D_m}{2}\right)}{\sin\left(\frac{A}{2}\right)}$. ($A$: angle of prism or apex angle).
      • For wavelength of monochromatic light diffraction grating is used: $d \sin\theta = m\lambda$.
      • Usually $m=1$ (first order diffraction).
      • $d$: grating element = $\frac{1}{N}$ ($N$: number of rulings per centimeter).

  • Speed of Light

    Show/Hide Details
    • First attempt to measure speed of light by Galileo.
    • First time speed measured by Michelson.
    • Michelson's formula: $c = 16fd$. ($f$: frequency of rotation of octagonal mirror, $d$: distance between plane mirror and octagonal mirror).

    • Index of Refraction (n)

      Show/Hide Details
      • Ratio of speed of light in vacuum to speed of light in medium.
      • $n = \frac{c}{v}$.

    • Snell's Law

      Show/Hide Details
      • Relates indices of refraction and angles of incidence/refraction.
      • $n_1 \sin\theta_1 = n_2 \sin\theta_2$.

  • Fibre Optics

    Show/Hide Details
    • Graham Bell invented photo phone to transmit voice message via beam of light.
    • Optical fiber: Light is used as a transmission carrier wave.
    • Principle: Total internal reflection.
    • Optical fibre is a fine glass rod having diameter in range of micrometers.

    • Classification of Optical Fibres

      Show/Hide Details
      • Single Mode Step Index Fibre: Mono mode fibre, very narrow core ($\sim 5~\mu m$).
      • Multimode Step Index Fibre: Larger core ($\sim 50~\mu m$), constant refractive index. Mode of transmission is total internal reflection. Useful for short distance.
      • Multimode Graded Index Fibre: Core diameter 50 to 1000 $\mu m$. No noticeable boundary between core and cladding. Mode of transmission is continuous refraction. Suitable for long distance transmission.

    • Signal Communication

      Show/Hide Details
      • Fibre optic communication system has three parts:
        • Transmitter: Converts electrical signals to light signals.
        • Optical Fibre: Guides the light signals.
        • Receiver: Captures the light signal and converts them to electric signals.
      • Light signals travel through fibre in 'on' and 'off' fashion (digital modulation).
      • Advantages:
        • Light signals travel faster than electrical signals in copper cable.
        • More messages per cable length (high signal strength).
        • Clear sound is received.
      • Losses in optical fibre:
        • Power loss due to scattering of light from impurity atoms in glass.
        • Power loss due to dispersion (can be reduced by graded index fibre).
        • Repeaters reduce power loss due to scattering from impurity atoms.
